Field Guide

Know the law. Identify illegal enforcement on the spot.

Important Note

Even if an operator is violating code(ones with a red border), CMPD usually treats this as a civil matter. You will likely have to pay to get your car back. Document everything and use our Legal Kit to file for a refund in small claims court.

Parking lots are private property, but enforcement must happen within the framework of Charlotte City Code and NC State Law.

This guide and project does not condone intentional non-compliance with parking rules. None of the above should encourage one to knowingly park where they should not.

1. Operator Regulations

Reference: Charlotte Code ยง 6-Article XI

Mandatory Identification(Sec. 6-564(a))

Upon request, the operator must provide company photo ID that includes:

  • Company Name
  • Company Phone Number
  • Company Physical Address

Vehicle Marking(Sec. 6-564(b))

Operators must use vehicles clearly marked with the company name, phone number, and address. Rogue operators often omit the address to avoid service of process.

Legal Entity Status(NCGS 66-71.4)

Operators must be a registered active LLC with the NC Secretary of State. Or using an registered assumed name, registered with the NC Secretary of State While the search page lists that typically, the status should confirmed with the official database before any action

Acceptable payment requirements for booters (Sec. 6-564(c))

A booting service must accept cash and at least two major credit and debit cards. The statute does not explicitly bar high(20%) fees, but is likely to be weighed as a unfair bus. practice by a small claims magistrate.

2. Fair Pricing(NCGS 75-1.1)

There is currently no hard cap on the books for what is considered reasonable fees in NC, after a 2011 CLT ordinance capping fees was overturned in 2016 by NC Supreme Court (King v. Town of Chapel Hill). Legitimate operators will charge no more than $150 for a boot with a fixed/narrow range, and 200 for a tow. Below board operators will often quote a wide range for the booting, often starting at $150, and going as high as $2000, which allows them to charge what they feel like. Unreasonable fees alone are unlikely to allow you to win a case on the grounds of unfair/deceptive business practices, but it is always worth a shot to make at the minimum a NCDOJ complaint to help raise awareness to a pattern.

3. Signage Scenarios

Scenario A

No Signage

Short-notice towing is illegal. If no signs are posted, a vehicle must generally be abandoned for 24+ hours or impeding traffic flow before action is taken. The business must manually contact the tow company and fill out a tow request.

Scenario B

The 24x24 Sign

Standard in South End/NoDa, this is where the predatory operators thrive. Signs must be 24"x24" and list the operator's name, address, phone, and specific fees. If any info is missing, they are in violation.

Some operators have been known to post in the lot with spotter cars in these lots. Upon owners leaving the property line, they pounce and boot. This practice is currently legal, but the operator performing the boot and collecting/removing the boot must still abide by CLT 6-564(a-b).

Scenario C

City Parking

These spots are denoted by smaller "No Parking", "2 HR Limit", or parkMobile, with "Call (704)336-7400" which is Charlotte 311. Enforcement is via tickets by City of charlotte employees. Towing is performed by SP+, but only in cases of multiple unpaid tickets, or impeding traffic flow.

Scenario D

Small non-standard Customer Only parking signs

Requires a business employee to manually call and sign for the tow. An operator lurking in these lots and towing without a signature is a violation.

Scenario E

Automated Plate Reader Surveillance Lot

These are seen in larger lots and decks, and can be identified by the cameras pointing into each entrance, with signs requesting you scan a QR to register your visit. Legal standing is currently ambigous, as Metropolis, a major operator of these lots recently settled with the state of TN $8.75m for deceptive business practices. They are unlikely to tow on sight, but will issue "fines" that if unpaid can have you booted/towed on sight from other company operated lots.
